Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning with Unsupervised Skill Discovery for Autonomous Driving

1Bilkent University

Abstract

Despite the years of work by technology companies and universities, autonomous driving is still a difficult problem to solve. Although some countries allow the use of autonomous vehicles in designated areas, this technology has not yet been widely accepted. One of the main reasons for this is the wide observation and decision space in which control algorithms operate. Deep reinforcement learning is one of the most promising candidates for solving this problem.

However, one of the weakest aspects of reinforcement learning methods is the need for a large number of iterations for learning. If the learning environment has sparse rewards (as is common in traffic) the number of required iterations increases even further. A solution to the sparse reward problem is the hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL). Lower-level policies are trained first with dense rewards independent of higher-level goals; then a higher-level policy learns how to select among them.

The contribution of this project is the development of an autonomous driving control framework based on unsupervised skill policies. Unlike human-designed policies, these skills are developed without human intervention, completely automatic, eliminating human bias. This enables the development of an autonomous driving algorithm that can be trained faster, achieves high performance, and behaves similarly to human drivers due to its hierarchical decision structure.

Simulation Environment

We build a comprehensive traffic simulator on top of the SUMO package, designed so that many distinct road topologies can be exercised within a single environment. In addition to the highway, on-ramp merge, and intersection scenarios outlined in the project proposal, we also added a roundabout scenario, providing the infrastructure needed to study the variety of traffic situations found in the literature.

Rather than hard-coding a handful of fixed traffic conditions, we exploit the flexibility of SUMO by defining traffic as a set of flows between road segments. Each flow is parameterized by its start and end edges, a mean speed and speed standard deviation, an initial vehicle count, and a per-step spawn probability. Because these parameters are exposed as plain configuration, large batches of experiments spanning many traffic-density and behavior combinations can be generated automatically and swept in simple loops.

By default, SUMO drives surrounding vehicles with the collision-free Krauss car-following model. While safe, this prevents our learning agent from ever experiencing the consequences of its own mistakes, weakening the reward signal. To address this we support two regimes: tuning SUMO's built-in safety parameters, and disabling the internal safety protocols entirely in favor of a custom surrounding-vehicle controller. The latter is a time-to-collision (TTC) based decision rule that chooses between maintain, accelerate, decelerate, and hard-decelerate actions, with stochastic accelerations sampled from Laplace and exponential distributions to mimic realistic, imperfect human driving.

Hierarchical Learning with Unsupervised Skill Discovery

At the lower level of the hierarchy, the agent autonomously discovers a repertoire of driving behaviors through unsupervised skill discovery, with no hand-designed objectives. We adopt DIAYN (Diversity Is All You Need), an information-theoretic framework that maximizes the mutual information between a sampled skill \(z\) and the states \(s\) the agent visits, while keeping actions as uninformative about the skill as possible:

\[ \max\; I(z; s) - I(a; z \mid s), \qquad I(z; s) = H(z) - H(z \mid s). \]

With a uniform prior over \(K\) skills, \(p(z) = 1/K\), the entropy \(H(z)\) is constant, so the objective reduces to minimizing \(H(z \mid s)\). This is realized by training a discriminator network \(q_\phi(z \mid s)\) that predicts which skill is active from the current state. Its log-probability defines the intrinsic reward given to the policy:

\[ r_z(s) = \log q_\phi(z \mid s) - \log p(z). \]

When the discriminator confidently and correctly identifies the active skill, \(q_\phi(z \mid s) \to 1\) and the reward attains its maximum value \(\log K\); an incorrect prediction drives the reward negative. This encourages every skill to seek out its own distinguishable region of the state space. The discriminator itself is trained in the opposite direction by minimizing the negative log-likelihood of the true skill label,

\[ \mathcal{L}_\phi = -\,\mathbb{E}_{s,z}\big[\log q_\phi(z \mid s)\big], \]

so that policy and discriminator form a mutually reinforcing loop. The skill policies are optimized with Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), whose entropy-regularized objective encourages exploration and prevents premature convergence:

\[ J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{t} \gamma^{t}\, r_z(s_t) + \alpha\, H\big(\pi(\cdot \mid s_t, z)\big)\right], \]

where \(\alpha\) is the temperature, \(\gamma\) the discount factor, and \(H(\pi)\) the policy entropy.

At each simulation step the agent receives an 11-dimensional normalized observation vector capturing its own speed, distances and relative speeds to the nearest leading and conflict-point vehicles, and the feasibility and geometry of a left lane change. From these observations each skill produces continuous acceleration and lane-change commands through a deep network. As a result, a skill is far richer than a simple "speed up when clear, slow down when blocked" rule: a single skill can, for example, accelerate on an open road while also yielding to a vehicle merging from the left. A higher-level policy then learns to invoke these complex skills in the right context to accomplish driving tasks.

Discovered Skills and Evaluation

We train and evaluate skill policies under two regimes. In the first, SUMO's internal safety protocols are disabled and surrounding vehicles follow our custom TTC-based controller, so the agent genuinely experiences collisions and the associated penalties. In the second, the default Krauss model is kept, placing the agent in a more controlled, collision-averse environment. In the Krauss setting we further separate training into light, medium, and dense traffic profiles, allowing us to study how skill discovery and behavioral separability change with traffic density.

Skills are assessed quantitatively, not just from video. For each skill \(z\) we measure the collision rate \(C_z\), the route-completion rate \(R_z\), the average speed \(\bar{v}_z\), and the minimum following distance \(d^{\min}_z\) over repeated episodes. These metrics reveal a clear safety–progress trade-off across the discovered skills: cautious skills achieve high completion rates and large following distances at lower speed, while aggressive skills reach the highest average speeds but collide far more often. One skill was so conservative that it almost never collided yet failed to complete any route.

Taken together, the results confirm that the DIAYN framework can produce a spectrum of skills with genuinely different speed–safety profiles. At the same time, a sizable subset of skills converged to similar behavior patterns, indicating that increasing behavioral diversity, tuning the number of skills, and re-training with different parameters are the key directions for the next reporting period.
